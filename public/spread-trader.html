The Concept: Mean Reversion & Pairs Trading

Two related stocks (e.g. two tech companies) tend to move together over time. Their spread (price difference) naturally drifts around a mean. When the spread stretches too far — measured by z-score (how many standard deviations from mean) — it tends to snap back. A z-score beyond ±2σ is a signal. The trade: sell the expensive one, buy the cheap one, and wait for reversion.

What is Z-Score?

z = (spread − mean) / std. If z = +2.5 it means the spread is 2.5 standard deviations above its mean — the spread is unusually wide, so we expect it to narrow. A z-score between −1 and +1 is "normal range" — no edge. Beyond ±2 is the trade zone.

How to Play

1

Watch two stocks: TechCo (A) and DataCo (B). The spread = A − B.

2

The z-score gauge below the chart shows the spread's position. Green zone = below −2σ (spread too narrow). Red zone = above +2σ (spread too wide).

3

LONG SPREAD when z < −2 (spread is compressed — buy A, sell B). SHORT SPREAD when z > +2 (spread is stretched — sell A, buy B).

4

Close when z returns near 0 (within ±0.5). You have 2 minutes. Score = realised P&L from spread moves.
